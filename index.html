Vectors vs. Scalars

A scalar has magnitude only (speed, mass, energy, temperature). A vector has both magnitude and direction (velocity, acceleration, force, displacement). Vectors are added using component methods: break each vector into x- and y-components using trigonometry, add the components separately, then recombine.

Vector Components
A_x = A cosθ
A_y = A sinθ
|A| = √(A_x² + A_y²)
θ = tan¹(A_y / A_x)
Significant figures: In multiplication/division, the answer has the same number of sig figs as the measurement with the fewest. In addition/subtraction, round to the least precise decimal place. All non-zero digits are significant. Zeros between non-zero digits are significant. Trailing zeros after a decimal are significant.

02Kinematics (1-D & 2-D)

Kinematics describes motion without worrying about what causes it. You need to know the key variables (displacement, velocity, acceleration, time) and the kinematic equations that relate them under constant acceleration.

Key Definitions

QuantityDefinitionUnitsScalar or Vector?
DistanceTotal path length traveledmScalar
Displacement (Δx)Change in position (straight-line, with direction)mVector
SpeedDistance / timem/sScalar
Velocity (v)Displacement / time (with direction)m/sVector
Acceleration (a)Change in velocity / timem/s²Vector
The Big Four Kinematic Equations (constant acceleration)
v = v₀ + at
Δx = v₀t + ½at²
v² = v₀² + 2aΔx
Δx = ½(v₀ + v)t

Free Fall

An object in free fall has acceleration equal to g = 9.8 m/s² downward (often rounded to 10 m/s²). Air resistance is ignored. An object dropped from rest has v₀ = 0. An object thrown straight up decelerates at g, stops momentarily at the peak, then accelerates back down at g. At the peak, v = 0 but a = g (still accelerating).

Projectile Motion (2-D)

Projectile motion is a combination of constant horizontal velocity and constant vertical acceleration (g downward). The horizontal and vertical components are independent of each other.

Projectile Motion
Horizontal: x = v₀cosθ · t (constant velocity, a_x = 0)
Vertical: y = v₀sinθ · t - ½gt² (accelerating at g)
Range (level ground): R = v₀² sin(2θ) / g
Remember
Maximum range occurs at 45° launch angle (on level ground). Complementary angles (e.g., 30° and 60°) give the same range but different flight times and peak heights.

03Newton's Laws & Forces

Newton's three laws form the foundation of classical mechanics. You need to know each law, how to draw free-body diagrams, and how to apply F = ma to solve problems.

The Three Laws

LawStatementKey Idea
1st (Inertia)An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted on by a net external force.No net force = no acceleration
2nd (F = ma)The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration.F_net = ma
3rd (Action-Reaction)For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.Forces come in pairs, on different objects
Newton's Second Law
ΣF = ma

Net force is the vector sum of all forces. If ΣF = 0, the object is in equilibrium (a = 0, which means at rest or constant velocity).

Common Forces

ForceSymbolDescription
WeightW = mgGravitational force, always directed downward. g = 9.8 m/s²
Normal forceN or F_NContact force perpendicular to the surface. Adjusts to prevent penetration.
TensionTPull force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable
FrictionfResists relative motion between surfaces in contact
Applied forceF_appAny external push or pull applied to the object
Spring forceF_s = -kxHooke's law: restoring force proportional to displacement
Free-body diagrams: Draw the object as a dot. Draw all forces acting ON the object as arrows starting from the dot, with length proportional to magnitude. Do not include forces the object exerts on other things. Label every force. Choose a coordinate system. This is the single most important problem-solving tool in mechanics.
Weight
W = mg
Mass (kg) is a measure of inertia. Weight (N) is the gravitational force. They are not the same. An astronaut has the same mass on the Moon but less weight because g is smaller.

04Friction, Inclines & Circular Motion

Friction

Friction opposes the relative motion (or tendency to move) between two surfaces in contact. Static friction prevents motion from starting. Kinetic friction acts on an object already sliding. Static friction can vary up to a maximum value; kinetic friction is roughly constant.

Friction Equations
f_s ≤ μ_s N (static, maximum)
f_k = μ_k N (kinetic)

μ_s is always greater than μ_k for the same pair of surfaces (it takes more force to start sliding than to keep sliding). N is the normal force, not necessarily equal to mg (depends on the situation).

Inclined Planes

On an incline at angle θ, you resolve weight into components parallel and perpendicular to the surface. The component pulling the object down the incline is mg sinθ. The normal force equals mg cosθ (on a frictionless incline with no other vertical forces).

Incline Components
F_parallel = mg sinθ (down the ramp)
F_perpendicular = mg cosθ (into the surface)
N = mg cosθ (on a simple incline)

Uniform Circular Motion

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is accelerating because its direction is constantly changing. The acceleration points toward the center of the circle (centripetal). The force causing this acceleration is centripetal force, which is not a new force but rather the net inward force (gravity, tension, friction, normal force, etc.).

Circular Motion
a_c = v² / r
F_c = mv² / r
Period: T = 2πr / v
There is no outward "centrifugal force" in physics. What you feel is your body's inertia resisting the inward acceleration. The only real force is centripetal (inward).

05Work, Energy & Power

Work and energy provide an alternative to Newton's laws for solving mechanics problems. Energy is a scalar, so you avoid vector decomposition. The work-energy theorem and conservation of energy are two of the most powerful tools in physics.

Work
W = Fd cosθ

W = work (J), F = force (N), d = displacement (m), θ = angle between force and displacement. If θ = 0°, W = Fd. If θ = 90°, W = 0 (force perpendicular to motion does no work). If θ > 90°, work is negative.

Types of Energy

TypeFormulaDescription
Kinetic energyKE = ½mv²Energy of motion
Gravitational PEPE = mghEnergy due to height above reference point
Elastic PEPE_s = ½kx²Energy stored in a compressed/stretched spring
Work-Energy Theorem
W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² - ½mv₀²

The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy.
Conservation of Energy
KE_i + PE_i + W_nc = KE_f + PE_f

If only conservative forces act (no friction, no applied force), W_nc = 0 and total mechanical energy is conserved: KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f.
Power
P = W / t = Fv
Units: watts (W). 1 horsepower = 746 W.
Remember
Conservative forces (gravity, springs) have associated PE and conserve mechanical energy. Nonconservative forces (friction, air resistance, applied pushes) change total mechanical energy. Friction always removes mechanical energy (converts to heat).

06Momentum & Collisions

Momentum is a measure of how hard it is to stop a moving object. Like energy, momentum is conserved in a closed system, making it extremely useful for collision and explosion problems.

Momentum
p = mv
Units: kg · m/s. Momentum is a vector (same direction as velocity).
Impulse-Momentum Theorem
J = FΔt = Δp = mv_f - mv_i

Impulse (J) is the change in momentum. A longer collision time means less force (this is why airbags and crumple zones work).
Conservation of Momentum
m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i = m₁v₁f + m₂v₂f

Total momentum before = total momentum after, provided no external net force acts on the system.

Types of Collisions

TypeMomentum Conserved?KE Conserved?Description
ElasticYesYesObjects bounce apart; no energy lost (ideal)
InelasticYesNoSome KE converted to heat/sound/deformation
Perfectly inelasticYesNo (max loss)Objects stick together after collision
Momentum is always conserved in all collision types (as long as no external net force). Kinetic energy is only conserved in perfectly elastic collisions. In a perfectly inelastic collision, the objects stick together: (m₁ + m₂)v_f = m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i.

07Rotational Motion & Torque

Rotational motion has analogues for every linear quantity. Angle replaces displacement, angular velocity replaces linear velocity, and torque replaces force. Understanding these parallels makes the topic much more manageable.

Rotational Variables

LinearRotationalSymbolUnit
Displacement (x)Angular displacementθradians (rad)
Velocity (v)Angular velocityωrad/s
Acceleration (a)Angular accelerationαrad/s²
Mass (m)Moment of inertiaIkg · m²
Force (F)TorqueτN · m
Momentum (p)Angular momentumLkg · m²/s
Torque
τ = rF sinθ

r = distance from the pivot (lever arm), F = force, θ = angle between r and F. Torque is maximized when force is perpendicular to the lever arm (θ = 90°).
Key Rotational Equations
Στ = Iα (Newton's 2nd law for rotation)
v = rω (linear-angular link)
a_t = rα (tangential acceleration)
KE_rot = ½Iω²
L = Iω (angular momentum)

Static Equilibrium

An object is in static equilibrium when it has zero net force (ΣF = 0) and zero net torque (Στ = 0). This means no translation and no rotation. You can choose any point as the pivot when summing torques.

Remember
Conservation of angular momentum: if no external torque acts, L = Iω is constant. When a spinning ice skater pulls her arms in, I decreases and ω increases to keep L the same.

08Gravity & Orbits

Newton's law of universal gravitation describes the attractive force between any two objects with mass. It explains everything from falling apples to planetary orbits.

Universal Gravitation
F = Gm₁m₂ / r²

G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N · m²/kg². r is measured center-to-center. Force is always attractive.
Gravitational Acceleration
g = GM / r²

At Earth's surface: g ≈ 9.8 m/s². As you move farther from Earth's center, g decreases.

Orbits

For a circular orbit, gravity provides the centripetal force: GMm/r² = mv²/r. This gives orbital velocity v = √(GM/r). Higher orbits are slower. A satellite in orbit is in continuous free fall, but its forward velocity keeps it from hitting the surface.

Kepler's Third Law
T² ∝ r³

The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius. For objects orbiting the same central body, T²/r³ is constant.

09Fluids & Pressure

Fluids include both liquids and gases. The physics of fluids centers on pressure, buoyancy, and the behavior of moving fluids.

Pressure
P = F / A
Units: pascal (Pa) = N/m². Atmospheric pressure ≈ 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa = 1 atm.
Fluid Pressure with Depth
P = P₀ + ρgh

ρ = fluid density (kg/m³), h = depth, P₀ = surface pressure. Pressure increases linearly with depth and depends only on depth, not container shape (Pascal's principle).

Buoyancy

Archimedes' Principle
F_b = ρ_fluid · V_displaced · g

The buoyant force equals the weight of the fluid displaced. An object floats if its average density is less than the fluid density.

Moving Fluids

Continuity Equation
A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
Where the pipe narrows, fluid speeds up (and vice versa). Volume flow rate is constant.
Bernoulli's Equation
P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

Where fluid speeds up, pressure drops (and vice versa). This explains airplane lift, shower curtain pull-in, and atomizer sprays.

10Temperature & Heat

Thermodynamics deals with heat, temperature, and energy transfer. Temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles. Heat is energy transferred due to a temperature difference.

Temperature Scales

Conversions
K = °C + 273.15
°C = (°F - 32) × 5/9

Absolute zero = 0 K = -273.15°C. Water freezes at 273 K (0°C), boils at 373 K (100°C).

Thermal Expansion

Linear Expansion
ΔL = αL₀ΔT
α = coefficient of linear expansion. Most solids expand when heated.

Heat Transfer

Specific Heat (no phase change)
Q = mcΔT
Q = heat (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat (J/kg·K), ΔT = temperature change. Water: c = 4186 J/kg·K (very high, which is why water resists temperature change).
Latent Heat (phase change)
Q = mL
L = latent heat of fusion (melting/freezing) or vaporization (boiling/condensing). During a phase change, temperature stays constant while energy is absorbed or released.

Laws of Thermodynamics

LawStatement
0thIf A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in equilibrium with C (basis for thermometers)
1stΔU = Q - W. Energy is conserved. Heat added to a system goes into internal energy or work done by the system.
2ndHeat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never the reverse. Entropy of an isolated system always increases.
3rdAbsolute zero can never be reached.
Three modes of heat transfer: conduction (direct contact, molecular collisions), convection (bulk fluid motion, warm rises), radiation (electromagnetic waves, no medium needed).

11Waves & Sound

A wave transports energy without transporting matter. You need to know the basic wave properties, the difference between transverse and longitudinal waves, and the key behaviors of sound.

Wave Properties

PropertySymbolDefinitionUnit
WavelengthλDistance between two consecutive identical points (crest to crest)m
FrequencyfNumber of complete cycles per secondHz (1/s)
PeriodTTime for one complete cycles
AmplitudeAMaximum displacement from equilibriumm
Wave speedvSpeed at which the wave pattern movesm/s
Wave Speed Equation
v = fλ
f = 1/T

Transverse vs. Longitudinal

TypeParticle MotionExamples
TransversePerpendicular to wave directionLight, waves on a string, S-waves
LongitudinalParallel to wave directionSound, P-waves, compression springs

Sound

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave that requires a medium (it cannot travel through a vacuum). Speed of sound in air at 20°C ≈ 343 m/s. Sound travels faster in solids than in liquids, and faster in liquids than in gases. Pitch corresponds to frequency (high frequency = high pitch). Loudness corresponds to amplitude.

Wave Phenomena

PhenomenonDescription
ReflectionWave bounces off a surface. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.
RefractionWave changes direction when entering a new medium (speed changes).
DiffractionWave bends around obstacles or through openings.
InterferenceTwo waves overlap. Constructive (in phase, amplitude adds). Destructive (out of phase, amplitude cancels).
Doppler effectApparent frequency change when source and observer are in relative motion. Approaching = higher pitch. Receding = lower pitch.
ResonanceA system vibrates with maximum amplitude when driven at its natural frequency.
Remember
Doppler effect: Think of an ambulance. As it approaches, sound waves compress (shorter λ, higher f, higher pitch). As it moves away, waves stretch (longer λ, lower f, lower pitch).
