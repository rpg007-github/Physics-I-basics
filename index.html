Vectors vs. Scalars

A scalar has magnitude only (speed, mass, energy, temperature). A vector has both magnitude and direction (velocity, acceleration, force, displacement). Vectors are added using component methods: break each vector into x- and y-components using trigonometry, add the components separately, then recombine.

Vector Components
A_x = A cosθ
A_y = A sinθ
|A| = √(A_x² + A_y²)
θ = tan⁻¹(A_y / A_x)
Significant figures: In multiplication/division, the answer has the same number of sig figs as the measurement with the fewest. In addition/subtraction, round to the least precise decimal place.
📝 Worked Example — Vector Components
A force of 50 N is applied at an angle of 30° above the horizontal. What are its horizontal and vertical components?
Step 1 — Identify the formulas: A_x = A cosθ and A_y = A sinθ.
Step 2 — Plug in: A_x = 50 × cos(30°) = 50 × 0.866 = 43.3 N (horizontal).
Step 3 — A_y = 50 × sin(30°) = 50 × 0.500 = 25.0 N (vertical).
✓ Answer: Horizontal component = 43.3 N. Vertical component = 25.0 N. Check: √(43.3² + 25.0²) ≈ 50 N. ✓

02Kinematics (1-D & 2-D)

Kinematics describes motion without worrying about what causes it. You need to know the key variables (displacement, velocity, acceleration, time) and the kinematic equations that relate them under constant acceleration.

Key Definitions

Kinematic quantities: definition, units, and whether each is a scalar or vector
QuantityDefinitionUnitsScalar or Vector?
DistanceTotal path length traveledmScalar
Displacement (Δx)Change in position (straight-line, with direction)mVector
SpeedDistance / timem/sScalar
Velocity (v)Displacement / time (with direction)m/sVector
Acceleration (a)Change in velocity / timem/s²Vector
The Big Four Kinematic Equations (constant acceleration)
v = v₀ + at
Δx = v₀t + ½at²
v² = v₀² + 2aΔx
Δx = ½(v₀ + v)t
📝 Worked Example — Kinematics (1-D)
A car starts from rest and accelerates at 3 m/s². How fast is it going after 5 seconds, and how far has it traveled?
Step 1 — List knowns: v₀ = 0, a = 3 m/s², t = 5 s. Find v and Δx.
Step 2 — Find velocity: v = v₀ + at = 0 + (3)(5) = 15 m/s.
Step 3 — Find displacement: Δx = v₀t + ½at² = 0 + ½(3)(25) = 37.5 m.
✓ Answer: The car is traveling at 15 m/s and has covered 37.5 m.

Free Fall

An object in free fall has acceleration equal to g = 9.8 m/s² downward (often rounded to 10 m/s²). Air resistance is ignored. An object dropped from rest has v₀ = 0. An object thrown straight up decelerates at g, stops momentarily at the peak, then accelerates back down at g. At the peak, v = 0 but a = g (still accelerating).

📝 Worked Example — Free Fall
A ball is dropped from a bridge 45 m above the water. How long does it take to hit the water, and how fast is it going when it does?
Step 1 — Knowns: v₀ = 0, a = g = 10 m/s² (downward), Δx = 45 m. Find t and v.
Step 2 — Find time: Δx = ½gt² → 45 = ½(10)t² → t² = 9 → t = 3 s.
Step 3 — Find final velocity: v = v₀ + at = 0 + (10)(3) = 30 m/s.
✓ Answer: It takes 3 seconds to fall and hits the water at 30 m/s (about 67 mph).

Projectile Motion (2-D)

Projectile motion is a combination of constant horizontal velocity and constant vertical acceleration (g downward). The horizontal and vertical components are independent of each other.

Projectile Motion
Horizontal: x = v₀cosθ · t (constant velocity, a_x = 0)
Vertical: y = v₀sinθ · t - ½gt² (accelerating at g)
Range (level ground): R = v₀² sin(2θ) / g
Remember
Maximum range occurs at 45° launch angle (on level ground). Complementary angles (e.g., 30° and 60°) give the same range but different flight times and peak heights.
📝 Worked Example — Projectile Motion
A ball is kicked at 20 m/s at 30° above the horizontal (g = 10 m/s²). How far does it travel horizontally before landing?
Step 1 — Use the range formula: R = v₀² sin(2θ) / g.
Step 2 — Plug in: R = (20)² × sin(60°) / 10 = 400 × 0.866 / 10 = 34.6 m.
Step 3 — Check: sin(2 × 30°) = sin(60°) = 0.866. This is less than sin(90°) = 1, so this isn't max range — that would require 45°.
✓ Answer: The ball lands approximately 34.6 m away horizontally.

03Newton's Laws & Forces

Newton's three laws form the foundation of classical mechanics. You need to know each law, how to draw free-body diagrams, and how to apply F = ma to solve problems.

The Three Laws

Newton's three laws of motion with statements and key ideas
LawStatementKey Idea
1st (Inertia)An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at constant velocity, unless acted on by a net external force.No net force = no acceleration
2nd (F = ma)The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration.F_net = ma
3rd (Action-Reaction)For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.Forces come in pairs, on different objects
Newton's Second Law
ΣF = ma

Net force is the vector sum of all forces. If ΣF = 0, the object is in equilibrium (a = 0, meaning at rest or constant velocity).

Common Forces

Common forces in mechanics with symbols and descriptions
ForceSymbolDescription
WeightW = mgGravitational force, always directed downward. g = 9.8 m/s²
Normal forceN or F_NContact force perpendicular to the surface. Adjusts to prevent penetration.
TensionTPull force transmitted through a string, rope, or cable
FrictionfResists relative motion between surfaces in contact
Applied forceF_appAny external push or pull applied to the object
Spring forceF_s = −kxHooke's law: restoring force proportional to displacement
Free-body diagrams: Draw the object as a dot. Draw all forces acting ON the object as arrows starting from the dot, with length proportional to magnitude. Do not include forces the object exerts on other things. Label every force. Choose a coordinate system. This is the single most important problem-solving tool in mechanics.
📝 Worked Example — Newton's 2nd Law
A 10 kg box sits on a frictionless surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: 40 N to the right and 15 N to the left. What is its acceleration?
Step 1 — Find the net force. Take right as positive: ΣF = 40 − 15 = 25 N (to the right).
Step 2 — Apply Newton's 2nd Law: a = ΣF / m = 25 / 10 = 2.5 m/s².
Step 3 — Direction: the net force is to the right, so acceleration is to the right.
✓ Answer: The box accelerates at 2.5 m/s² to the right.

04Friction, Inclines & Circular Motion

Friction

Friction opposes the relative motion (or tendency to move) between two surfaces in contact. Static friction prevents motion from starting. Kinetic friction acts on an object already sliding. Static friction can vary up to a maximum value; kinetic friction is roughly constant.

Friction Equations
f_s ≤ μ_s N (static, maximum)
f_k = μ_k N (kinetic)

μ_s is always greater than μ_k for the same pair of surfaces. N is the normal force, not necessarily equal to mg.
📝 Worked Example — Friction
A 20 kg crate rests on a horizontal floor. The coefficient of static friction is 0.40 and kinetic friction is 0.30. What force is needed to start it moving? What force keeps it moving at constant speed?
Step 1 — Find the normal force. On a flat surface: N = mg = 20 × 9.8 = 196 N.
Step 2 — To start moving, you must overcome maximum static friction: f_s(max) = μ_s × N = 0.40 × 196 = 78.4 N.
Step 3 — To keep it moving at constant speed (a = 0), the applied force equals kinetic friction: f_k = μ_k × N = 0.30 × 196 = 58.8 N.
✓ Answer: You need more than 78.4 N to start it, but only 58.8 N to keep it sliding at constant speed.

Inclined Planes

On an incline at angle θ, you resolve weight into components parallel and perpendicular to the surface.

Incline Components
F_parallel = mg sinθ (down the ramp)
F_perpendicular = mg cosθ (into the surface)
N = mg cosθ (on a simple incline)
📝 Worked Example — Inclined Plane
A 5 kg block sits on a frictionless ramp angled at 37° (sin 37° = 0.60, cos 37° = 0.80, g = 10 m/s²). What is its acceleration down the ramp?
Step 1 — The only force along the ramp (no friction) is the parallel component of gravity: F_parallel = mg sinθ = 5 × 10 × 0.60 = 30 N.
Step 2 — Apply F = ma along the ramp: 30 = 5 × a → a = 6 m/s².
Step 3 — Note: the normal force is N = mg cosθ = 5 × 10 × 0.80 = 40 N, but it does no work (perpendicular to motion).
✓ Answer: The block accelerates at 6 m/s² down the ramp.

Uniform Circular Motion

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is accelerating because its direction is constantly changing. The acceleration points toward the center of the circle (centripetal).

Circular Motion
a_c = v² / r
F_c = mv² / r
Period: T = 2πr / v
There is no outward "centrifugal force" in physics. What you feel is your body's inertia resisting the inward acceleration. The only real force is centripetal (inward).
📝 Worked Example — Circular Motion
A 0.5 kg ball is swung in a horizontal circle of radius 1.2 m at 4 m/s. What centripetal force is needed?
Step 1 — Use F_c = mv² / r.
Step 2 — Plug in: F_c = (0.5)(4)² / 1.2 = (0.5)(16) / 1.2 = 8 / 1.2 = 6.67 N.
Step 3 — This force is provided by the tension in the string, directed inward toward the center.
✓ Answer: The string must exert approximately 6.67 N of tension toward the center.

05Work, Energy & Power

Work and energy provide an alternative to Newton's laws for solving mechanics problems. Energy is a scalar, so you avoid vector decomposition. The work-energy theorem and conservation of energy are two of the most powerful tools in physics.

Work
W = Fd cosθ

W = work (J), F = force (N), d = displacement (m), θ = angle between force and displacement. If θ = 90°, W = 0 (force perpendicular to motion does no work). If θ > 90°, work is negative.

Types of Energy

Types of mechanical energy with formulas and descriptions
TypeFormulaDescription
Kinetic energyKE = ½mv²Energy of motion
Gravitational PEPE = mghEnergy due to height above reference point
Elastic PEPE_s = ½kx²Energy stored in a compressed/stretched spring
Work-Energy Theorem
W_net = ΔKE = ½mv² − ½mv₀²
Conservation of Energy
KE_i + PE_i + W_nc = KE_f + PE_f

If only conservative forces act (no friction, no applied force), W_nc = 0 and total mechanical energy is conserved.
Power
P = W / t = Fv
Units: watts (W). 1 horsepower = 746 W.
📝 Worked Example — Conservation of Energy
A 2 kg ball is dropped from a height of 5 m. What is its speed just before it hits the ground? (ignore air resistance, g = 10 m/s²)
Step 1 — Set the ground as the reference level (PE = 0 at ground). At the top: KE_i = 0 (dropped from rest), PE_i = mgh = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100 J.
Step 2 — At the bottom: PE_f = 0. By conservation of energy: KE_f = PE_i = 100 J.
Step 3 — Solve for v: ½mv² = 100 → ½(2)v² = 100 → v² = 100 → v = 10 m/s.
✓ Answer: The ball hits the ground at 10 m/s. (Notice you didn't need kinematics at all!)

06Momentum & Collisions

Momentum is a measure of how hard it is to stop a moving object. Like energy, momentum is conserved in a closed system, making it extremely useful for collision and explosion problems.

Momentum
p = mv
Units: kg · m/s. Momentum is a vector (same direction as velocity).
Impulse-Momentum Theorem
J = FΔt = Δp = mv_f − mv_i

A longer collision time means less force (this is why airbags and crumple zones work).
Conservation of Momentum
m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i = m₁v₁f + m₂v₂f

Types of Collisions

Types of collisions and what is conserved in each
TypeMomentum Conserved?KE Conserved?Description
ElasticYesYesObjects bounce apart; no energy lost (ideal)
InelasticYesNoSome KE converted to heat/sound/deformation
Perfectly inelasticYesNo (max loss)Objects stick together after collision
Momentum is always conserved in all collision types (as long as no external net force). In a perfectly inelastic collision: (m₁ + m₂)v_f = m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i.
📝 Worked Example — Perfectly Inelastic Collision
A 1,000 kg car moving at 12 m/s collides with a stationary 500 kg car and they stick together. What is their combined velocity after the collision?
Step 1 — Use conservation of momentum: m₁v₁i + m₂v₂i = (m₁ + m₂)v_f.
Step 2 — Plug in: (1000)(12) + (500)(0) = (1000 + 500)v_f → 12,000 = 1,500 v_f.
Step 3 — Solve: v_f = 12,000 / 1,500 = 8 m/s.
✓ Answer: The combined wreck moves at 8 m/s in the original direction. Momentum was conserved; kinetic energy was not (lost to crumpling metal and heat).

07Rotational Motion & Torque

Rotational motion has analogues for every linear quantity. Understanding these parallels makes the topic much more manageable.

Rotational Variables

Rotational analogues of linear motion quantities
LinearRotationalSymbolUnit
Displacement (x)Angular displacementθradians (rad)
Velocity (v)Angular velocityωrad/s
Acceleration (a)Angular accelerationαrad/s²
Mass (m)Moment of inertiaIkg · m²
Force (F)TorqueτN · m
Momentum (p)Angular momentumLkg · m²/s
Torque
τ = rF sinθ

r = distance from the pivot (lever arm), F = force, θ = angle between r and F. Torque is maximized when force is perpendicular to the lever arm (θ = 90°).
Key Rotational Equations
Στ = Iα (Newton's 2nd law for rotation)
v = rω (linear-angular link)
a_t = rα (tangential acceleration)
KE_rot = ½Iω²
L = Iω (angular momentum)

Static Equilibrium

An object is in static equilibrium when it has zero net force (ΣF = 0) and zero net torque (Στ = 0).

Remember
Conservation of angular momentum: if no external torque acts, L = Iω is constant. When a spinning ice skater pulls her arms in, I decreases and ω increases to keep L the same.
📝 Worked Example — Torque & Static Equilibrium
A uniform 4 m beam weighing 200 N is supported at its left end by a wall and by a cable attached 3 m from the left end. A 300 N sign hangs from the right end. What is the tension T in the cable?
Step 1 — For static equilibrium, Στ = 0. Choose the left end (wall) as the pivot so the wall force creates no torque.
Step 2 — Clockwise torques (tending to rotate beam down): beam weight at center (2 m) = 200 × 2 = 400 N·m; sign at right end (4 m) = 300 × 4 = 1200 N·m. Total clockwise = 1600 N·m.
Step 3 — Counter-clockwise torque: cable tension T at 3 m from pivot = T × 3. Set equal to clockwise: 3T = 1600 → T = 533 N.
✓ Answer: The cable tension is approximately 533 N. Notice how choosing the pivot at the wall eliminated one unknown from the equation.

08Gravity & Orbits

Newton's law of universal gravitation describes the attractive force between any two objects with mass. It explains everything from falling apples to planetary orbits.

Universal Gravitation
F = Gm₁m₂ / r²

G = 6.674 × 10⁻¹¹ N · m²/kg². r is measured center-to-center. Force is always attractive.
Gravitational Acceleration
g = GM / r²

At Earth's surface: g ≈ 9.8 m/s². As you move farther from Earth's center, g decreases.

Orbits

For a circular orbit, gravity provides the centripetal force: GMm/r² = mv²/r. This gives orbital velocity v = √(GM/r). Higher orbits are slower. A satellite in orbit is in continuous free fall, but its forward velocity keeps it from hitting the surface.

Kepler's Third Law
T² ∝ r³

The square of the orbital period is proportional to the cube of the orbital radius. For objects orbiting the same central body, T²/r³ is constant.
📝 Worked Example — Inverse-Square Law
Earth's gravity pulls you with force F at Earth's surface. If you travel to a distance of 2 Earth radii from Earth's center, how does the gravitational force change?
Step 1 — Gravitational force follows F = Gm₁m₂ / r². So F ∝ 1/r².
Step 2 — If r doubles (from 1 R_E to 2 R_E), then r² increases by a factor of 4.
Step 3 — Force becomes F/4 — one quarter as strong.
✓ Answer: The gravitational force is reduced to 1/4 of its surface value. If you weigh 600 N at Earth's surface, you'd weigh 150 N at 2 Earth radii.

09Fluids & Pressure

Fluids include both liquids and gases. The physics of fluids centers on pressure, buoyancy, and the behavior of moving fluids.

Pressure
P = F / A
Units: pascal (Pa) = N/m². Atmospheric pressure ≈ 1.013 × 10⁵ Pa = 1 atm.
Fluid Pressure with Depth
P = P₀ + ρgh

ρ = fluid density (kg/m³), h = depth, P₀ = surface pressure. Pressure increases linearly with depth (Pascal's principle).

Buoyancy

Archimedes' Principle
F_b = ρ_fluid · V_displaced · g

The buoyant force equals the weight of the fluid displaced. An object floats if its average density is less than the fluid density.
📝 Worked Example — Buoyancy
A 0.002 m³ block of wood (ρ = 600 kg/m³) is fully submerged in water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³). Will it sink or float? What is the net force on it?
Step 1 — Weight of wood: W = ρ_wood × V × g = 600 × 0.002 × 10 = 12 N (downward).
Step 2 — Buoyant force: F_b = ρ_water × V × g = 1000 × 0.002 × 10 = 20 N (upward).
Step 3 — Net force = 20 − 12 = 8 N upward. The wood accelerates upward and will float.
✓ Answer: The wood floats. The buoyant force (20 N) exceeds its weight (12 N) because wood is less dense than water.

Moving Fluids

Continuity Equation
A₁v₁ = A₂v₂
Where the pipe narrows, fluid speeds up. Volume flow rate is constant.
Bernoulli's Equation
P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant

Where fluid speeds up, pressure drops. This explains airplane lift, shower curtain pull-in, and atomizer sprays.

10Temperature & Heat

Thermodynamics deals with heat, temperature, and energy transfer. Temperature measures average kinetic energy of particles. Heat is energy transferred due to a temperature difference.

Temperature Scales

Conversions
K = °C + 273.15
°C = (°F − 32) × 5/9

Absolute zero = 0 K = −273.15°C. Water freezes at 273 K (0°C), boils at 373 K (100°C).

Heat Transfer

Specific Heat (no phase change)
Q = mcΔT
Q = heat (J), m = mass (kg), c = specific heat (J/kg·K), ΔT = temperature change. Water: c = 4186 J/kg·K.
Latent Heat (phase change)
Q = mL
L = latent heat of fusion or vaporization. During a phase change, temperature stays constant.
📝 Worked Example — Specific Heat
How much heat is needed to raise 2 kg of water from 20°C to 80°C? (c_water = 4186 J/kg·K)
Step 1 — Identify: no phase change occurs (stays liquid). Use Q = mcΔT.
Step 2 — ΔT = 80 − 20 = 60°C (same as 60 K for a difference).
Step 3 — Q = (2)(4186)(60) = 502,320 J ≈ 502 kJ.
✓ Answer: About 502 kJ of heat is needed. This shows why water takes so long to boil — its specific heat is very high.

Laws of Thermodynamics

The four laws of thermodynamics and their statements
LawStatement
0thIf A is in thermal equilibrium with B, and B with C, then A is in equilibrium with C (basis for thermometers)
1stΔU = Q − W. Energy is conserved. Heat added to a system goes into internal energy or work done by the system.
2ndHeat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never the reverse. Entropy of an isolated system always increases.
3rdAbsolute zero can never be reached.
Three modes of heat transfer: conduction (direct contact), convection (bulk fluid motion, warm rises), radiation (electromagnetic waves, no medium needed).

11Waves & Sound

A wave transports energy without transporting matter. You need to know the basic wave properties, the difference between transverse and longitudinal waves, and the key behaviors of sound.

Wave Properties

Wave properties with symbols, definitions, and units
PropertySymbolDefinitionUnit
WavelengthλDistance between two consecutive identical points (crest to crest)m
FrequencyfNumber of complete cycles per secondHz (1/s)
PeriodTTime for one complete cycles
AmplitudeAMaximum displacement from equilibriumm
Wave speedvSpeed at which the wave pattern movesm/s
Wave Speed Equation
v = fλ
f = 1/T

Transverse vs. Longitudinal

Comparison of transverse and longitudinal waves
TypeParticle MotionExamples
TransversePerpendicular to wave directionLight, waves on a string, S-waves
LongitudinalParallel to wave directionSound, P-waves, compression springs

Sound

Sound is a longitudinal mechanical wave that requires a medium (it cannot travel through a vacuum). Speed of sound in air at 20°C ≈ 343 m/s. Sound travels faster in solids than in liquids, and faster in liquids than in gases.

📝 Worked Example — Wave Speed
A sound wave in air has a frequency of 440 Hz (musical note A). What is its wavelength? (speed of sound = 343 m/s)
Step 1 — Use v = fλ, rearranged to λ = v / f.
Step 2 — λ = 343 / 440 = 0.780 m (about 78 cm).
Step 3 — Notice: higher-frequency sounds have shorter wavelengths. A 4400 Hz sound would have λ = 0.078 m.
✓ Answer: The wavelength is approximately 0.78 m.

Wave Phenomena

Wave phenomena and descriptions
PhenomenonDescription
ReflectionWave bounces off a surface. Angle of incidence = angle of reflection.
RefractionWave changes direction when entering a new medium (speed changes).
DiffractionWave bends around obstacles or through openings.
InterferenceTwo waves overlap. Constructive (in phase, amplitude adds). Destructive (out of phase, amplitude cancels).
Doppler effectApparent frequency change when source and observer are in relative motion. Approaching = higher pitch. Receding = lower pitch.
ResonanceA system vibrates with maximum amplitude when driven at its natural frequency.
Remember
Doppler effect: Think of an ambulance. As it approaches, sound waves compress (shorter λ, higher f, higher pitch). As it moves away, waves stretch (longer λ, lower f, lower pitch).
